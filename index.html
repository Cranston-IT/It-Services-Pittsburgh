About Pittsburgh

Pittsburgh (/ˈpɪtsbɜːrɡ/ PITS-burg) serves as the county seat of Allegheny County, Pennsylvania, and is the second-most populous city in the state after Philadelphia. With a population of 302,971 as of the 2020 census, it ranks as the 68th-most populous city in the United States. Situated in southwestern Pennsylvania, Pittsburgh lies at the confluence of the Allegheny and Monongahela Rivers, which merge to form the Ohio River. The city anchors the Pittsburgh metropolitan area, home to 2.457 million residents, making it the largest metro area in the Ohio Valley and Appalachia, the second-largest in Pennsylvania, and the 26th-largest in the nation. Pittsburgh is also the primary city within the greater Pittsburgh–Weirton–Steubenville combined statistical area, which includes parts of Ohio and West Virginia.

Known as "The Steel City," Pittsburgh played a crucial role in the U.S. steel industry's history. Its strategic location, rich in resources from the Allegheny Mountains, made it a contested region among the French, British, Virginians, Whiskey Rebels, and Civil War raiders. During the early 20th century, Pittsburgh ranked behind only New York City and Chicago in corporate headquarters employment and boasted the highest number of U.S. stockholders per capita. However, deindustrialization in the late 20th century brought significant layoffs in steel and heavy industries, leading to economic decline and the relocation of several major corporations. Since the 1990s, Pittsburgh has successfully transitioned from its steel legacy to focus on healthcare, education, and technology, emerging as a leader in these sectors.
